> Friends are chosen with the heart, not the head. They expect favoritism, grow envious of your success, and can betray you out of resentment once you can no longer give them what they want. A former enemy, brought into your circle, has everything to prove — they will often work harder, stay more loyal, and be more useful. Hire a former enemy and you make a friend; hire a friend and you often make an enemy.

## Transgression of the Law — Michael III & Basilius

Michael III, Emperor of Byzantium, was a man of generous impulse. When he came across Basilius — a young peasant of remarkable physical strength and charm — he was immediately taken with him. He elevated Basilius from a common stable hand to his most trusted companion, showering him with titles, wealth, and eventually naming him co-emperor. No favor was too great for his friend.

What Michael failed to see was that Basilius was not driven by loyalty — he was driven by ambition. A friend given unlimited access and power has nothing left to earn. His gratitude curdles into expectation, and expectation eventually curdles into resentment. Basilius, having reached the highest office a man could hold short of sole ruler, saw one final obstacle between himself and the throne: Michael himself.

In 867 AD, Basilius had the Emperor assassinated and seized sole power, founding the Macedonian dynasty — one of Byzantium's most enduring royal lines.

Michael's only crime was trusting too completely in the bond of friendship, forgetting that power operates by its own rules — ones that sentiment cannot override.

## Observance of the Law — Emperor Sung

When the first Sung Emperor consolidated power over China, he faced a familiar dilemma: who could he trust to help him govern a vast and fractious empire? His instinct, like most rulers, was to turn to friends and allies who had supported him during his rise.

Instead, he made the counterintuitive move of reaching out to former adversaries — warlords and generals who had once opposed him. He offered them titles, land, and honorable positions within his court, effectively converting enemies into stakeholders in his own success.

The results were revealing. His old friends, assuming their loyalty entitled them to comfort and leniency, became complacent and politically demanding. His former enemies, acutely aware that they had once been on the wrong side, worked with relentless dedication to prove their worth and secure their position. They had no room for complacency — one misstep and they knew how quickly they could fall from favor.

By keeping former enemies close and giving them something to prove, Sung created a court of capable, motivated, and surprisingly dependable allies — bound not by sentiment, but by self-interest, which is a far more reliable adhesive.

## Summary

- Friends blur the line between personal loyalty and professional judgment — they expect special treatment whether they deserve it or not
- A friend given power feels entitled; a former enemy given power feels grateful — and works to stay that way
- Enemies make better allies precisely because the stakes are higher for them — they have something to prove and nothing to take for granted
- Sentimentality is a liability in the arena of power; relationships should be evaluated by usefulness, not history
- This law is not about being cold — it is about being clear-eyed; keep friends close personally, but never let friendship alone determine who earns your trust professionally
- The most dangerous betrayals almost always come from those closest to you — not because people are evil, but because intimacy breeds expectation, and expectation breeds disappointment
