> Always make those above you feel comfortablly superior. In your desire to please and impress them, don not go too far in displaying your talents or you might accomplish the opposite - inspire fear and insecurity. Make your master appear more brilliant than they are and you will attain the highest of the powers. 

## Transgression of the Law
Nicolas Fouquet, the Finance Minister of Louis XIV, was a man of grand taste - famous for lavish parties and magnetic social presence. For years, he enjoyed a comfortable position in the King's favor.

When he sensed that favor slipping, he made a fateful miscalculation. To win the King back, he threw the most spectacular party France had ever seen — held at his own magnificent estate, Vaux-le-Vicomte. He spared nothing: an exquisite six-course banquet, hundreds of guests, theatrical performances, and a breathtaking fireworks display, all held in the King's honor.

But Louis XIV was himself a man of immense showmanship and pride. Rather than feeling flattered, he felt humiliated — outshone on his own stage, in his own kingdom, by his own servant. The sheer grandeur of Fouquet's estate made the royal palace look modest by comparison. Within days, the King had Fouquet arrested on charges of embezzling royal funds. He spent the rest of his life in prison.

His only true crime was outshining the master.
## Observance of the Law
Galileo lived the perpetual anxiety of a scientist dependent on wealthy patrons to fund his research. Securing sponsorship was a constant, exhausting negotiation that distracted from his work and left him vulnerable.

When he discovered the four moons of Jupiter in 1610, he recognized an opportunity that went beyond science. Rather than simply publishing his findings and taking sole credit, he made a masterful political move: he named the moons the _Medicean Stars_, directly linking his discovery to Cosimo de' Medici II, the Grand Duke of Tuscany. He then dedicated his published work, _Sidereus Nuncius_, to Cosimo, framing the celestial discovery as a reflection of the Medici family's own greatness — as if the heavens themselves were announcing their glory.

The effect was immediate. Cosimo was flattered beyond measure. Galileo was appointed the official Court Philosopher and Mathematician of the Medici, granted a generous salary, and freed from the burden of chasing patronage for the rest of his career.

He achieved this not by hiding his brilliance, but by _directing_ it — ensuring that his talent illuminated his patron rather than casting him in shadow.

## Summary of the Law
- Never outshine those in power — their ego matters more than your brilliance in the moment
- Displaying too much talent upward creates insecurity, not admiration
- Make superiors feel smarter, more capable, and in control
- Subtlety beats display; let your strengths _serve_ them, not compete with them
- True power often comes from making the right people comfortable, not from being the most impressive person in the room
- The goal is not to shrink yourself, but to _redirect_ your shine so it reflects well on those above you
